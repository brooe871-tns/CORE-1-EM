Matterhorn Highlights Trek

Growing up in Switzerland, there is a photo that I hold close to my heart — me, less than a year old, perched in front of the beautiful silhouette of this mountain. Although I have never done the Matterhorn Highlights Trek, I know one day that I will return to the country where I was born and complete it — it's one of my lifelong dreams to see this special spot from as many angles as possible and continue to absorb, understand and appreciate the beauty of both its and my country. The Matterhorn is one of the most iconic peaks in the Alps, a pyramidal giant straddling the border of Switzerland and Italy whose silhouette has shaped mountaineering history. Though surrounded by a vast network of trails, the paths encircling and approaching the Matterhorn are known for their demanding alpine terrain, dramatic exposure, and constantly shifting weather. According to many hikers I’ve spoken to—and countless accounts passed down by seasoned trekkers—the area is breathtaking in a way that photographs never quite capture: glaciers draped across ridgelines, high-altitude meadows scattered with slate-roofed hamlets, and the mountain’s sharp, unmistakable profile dominating the horizon. Despite how famous it is, the region still carries traces of its earlier past, when these routes existed mainly for seasonal movement, trade, and shepherding rather than recreation. Long before tourism transformed Zermatt and Cervinia, locals used these paths to move livestock, transport goods, and cross valleys isolated by snow for much of the year. Modern trails continue this lineage, but with updated markings, mountain huts, and conservation efforts that aim to protect both the alpine environment and the cultural traditions of high-mountain life. Today, trekking near the Matterhorn feels like walking through layered histories—glaciological, cultural, and mountaineering—all converging around one of the most legendary summits on Earth.

50km. 5 days.
